How do proteins get folded?

As we learned in Dr. Babu's class, proteins must be folded in order to fulfill their intended function. However, protein folding is a very complicated process. Proteins get folded almost randomly in the cell by bumping into water molecules and other foces until they take on their final shape and can be used. What can folding simulations tell us?

As "The protein folding revolution" video says, understanding protein folding enables us to design new proteins to be used for delivering drugs, creating new materials, and more. In addition to designing new proteins, we can also get more information about protein misfolding and associated diseases, including sickle-cell anemia, type II diabetes, and cystic fibrosis.

How can we get enough compute power?

Running these simulations is very computationally expensive and cannot be done efficiently on one computer. However, the problem can be split into multiple pieces which can be simulated on multiple computers at the same time. However, several powerful computers is still not powerful enough to run large scale research projects. This is where crowd-sourced solutions come in: you can donate cpu power to run folding simulations for research!

Folding@home

Folding@home is a distributed computing project for disease research that simulates protein folding, computational drug design, and other types of molecular dynamics. It is based in Stanford University at the Pande Labratory and has been running since October 1, 2000. It relies on donated CPU power, and anyone can contribute. Since the beginning, they have released papers based on crowdsourced data on antibiotics, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, Huntington's, and more. Folding@home has a desktop app which you can install, and if you're using Google Chrome, you can contribute without even installing the app here.

Rosetta@home

Rosetta@home is a project similar to Folding@home, but is run by the University of Washington. Results from Rosetta@home calculations have been used to model proteins in herpes simplex virus 1, design possible vaccines for HIV, and design proteins to fight against malaria.
